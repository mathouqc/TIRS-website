# History of Astronomy
## Part 2: From Galileo to the Modern Era

---

### Tycho Brahe: the observer

**Tycho Brahe** (1546-1601) was the greatest astronomical observer of the pre-telescopic era. Thanks to funding from the King of Denmark, he built the Uraniborg observatory on the island of Hven and collected twenty years of planetary position measurements with unprecedented precision - better than one arcminute.

Tycho could not bring himself to accept the Copernican model: he detected no stellar parallax (the evidence he expected for Earth's motion), and the idea of a heavy moving Earth seemed physically absurd to him. He proposed a hybrid model, now called *Tychonic*: the planets orbit the Sun, but the Sun itself orbits an immobile Earth. This model produced the same geometric predictions as Copernicus while keeping Earth at the center.

His data remained the most valuable resource in astronomy at the time. After his death, they passed to his assistant.

---

### Kepler: the laws of planetary motion

**Johannes Kepler** (1571-1630), Tycho Brahe's assistant and a brilliant mathematician, inherited these data and worked to reconcile them with the Copernican model. After years of intense calculations, he discovered that planets move not in circles but in **ellipses**, with the Sun at one focus.

He formulated his three laws between 1609 and 1619:

1. **First law (elliptical orbits)**: The orbit of each planet is an ellipse with the Sun at one of the two foci.

2. **Second law (equal areas)**: The line connecting the Sun to a planet sweeps out equal areas in equal times. A planet therefore moves faster when it is near the Sun (perihelion) and slower when it is farther away (aphelion).

3. **Third law (periods)**: The square of a planet's orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis of its orbit. Mathematically: $T^2 \\propto a^3$.

For the first time, these laws described the Solar System with excellent precision and no epicycles. They formed the foundation on which Newton built celestial mechanics half a century later.

---

### Galileo and the telescope

**Galileo Galilei** (1564-1642) did not invent the telescope, but he was the first to systematically point it at the sky and publish the results in *Sidereus Nuncius* (1610).

His discoveries were revolutionary:

- **Mountains and craters on the Moon**: the lunar surface was not perfectly smooth and spherical as Aristotelian physics claimed, but rough and Earth-like.
- **Jupiter's four large moons** (Io, Europa, Ganymede, Callisto): celestial bodies orbiting another object than Earth, refuting the idea that everything must orbit Earth.
- **Phases of Venus**: Venus showed a complete cycle of phases, similar to the Moon, which can only be explained if Venus orbits the Sun - incompatible with the Ptolemaic model.
- **The Milky Way**: resolved into an immense number of individual stars, suggesting a far larger universe than previously imagined.

Galileo publicly defended Copernicanism, which brought him into conflict with the Catholic Church. In 1633, he was condemned by the Inquisition and forced to recant. He spent the last years of his life under house arrest. The Church officially rehabilitated Galileo in 1992.

---

### Newton: universal gravitation

**Isaac Newton** (1643-1727) achieved the theoretical synthesis that Kepler's work demanded. In *Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica* (1687), he formulated the **law of universal gravitation**:

$$
F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
$$

Every mass attracts every other mass with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of their distance. Newton showed that this single law could derive Kepler's three laws - planetary motion, falling bodies, and tides all obeyed the same principle.

Newtonian mechanics dominated physics for more than two centuries. It made it possible to predict the return of Halley's comet (1758), discover Neptune through calculation in 1846 before direct observation, and still today to plan spacecraft trajectories.

---

### The 18th and 19th centuries: mapping the universe

Over the following centuries, improvements in instruments greatly expanded knowledge of the cosmos.

**William Herschel** (1738-1822) built the largest telescopes of his time and discovered Uranus in 1781 - the first planet discovered with an optical instrument. He cataloged thousands of nebulae and star clusters and tried to map the shape of the Milky Way.

**Spectroscopy**, developed from the 1850s by Bunsen, Kirchhoff, and Fraunhofer, opened a radically new window: by analyzing starlight, astronomers could now determine chemical composition, temperature, and radial velocity (Doppler effect). Physical astronomy, or **astrophysics**, was born.

**Henrietta Swan Leavitt** (1868-1921) discovered the period-luminosity relation of Cepheid stars (1908-1912), giving astronomers the first reliable rule to measure distances to faraway galaxies - the "standard candles" of extragalactic astronomy.

---

### The 20th century: an expanding universe

**Edwin Hubble** (1889-1953) used Cepheids to show that some nebulae, including Andromeda, lie far beyond the Milky Way: they are entire galaxies, "island universes." In 1929, he published his expansion law: galaxies move away from us at a speed proportional to their distance,

$$
v = H_0 \, d
$$

all galaxies move away from each other, which implies that the universe is expanding. Tracing this backward leads to an initial extremely dense and hot state: the **Big Bang**.

Einstein's **general relativity** (1915) provided the theoretical framework for this cosmology. The discovery of the cosmic microwave background in 1964 by Penzias and Wilson decisively confirmed the Big Bang model.

The end of the 20th century brought a major surprise: in 1998, two independent teams studying distant supernovae showed that the universe's expansion is not slowing down - it is **accelerating**. This acceleration is attributed to mysterious **dark energy**, which accounts for about 68% of the universe's total energy content. Its nature remains one of the major open questions in modern physics.

---

### Astronomy today

Modern astronomy uses ground- and space-based observatories across the full electromagnetic spectrum: radio, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X-rays, and gamma rays. Since 2015, gravitational-wave detectors such as LIGO and Virgo have opened an entirely new window on the cosmos.

Telescopes such as Hubble (launched in 1990) and its successor James Webb (2021) have pushed observation limits back to the first galaxies formed after the Big Bang. Planetary missions have transformed our knowledge of the Solar System.

After millennia of naked-eye observation, a few centuries of telescopes, and a few decades of space exploration, astronomy has taken us from an immobile Earth at the center of a small cosmos to a universe of hundreds of billions of galaxies, each containing hundreds of billions of stars. This intellectual journey, marked by revolutions and resistance, is one of the most remarkable odysseys of the human mind.
