# History of Astronomy
## Part 1: From Origins to the Renaissance

---

### The first observations

Astronomy is one of humanity's oldest sciences. Long before writing, prehistoric peoples observed the night sky and looked for patterns and landmarks. The stone alignments at Stonehenge in England (around 3000 BCE) show a precise knowledge of solar and lunar cycles.

Early agricultural civilizations had a direct practical interest in astronomy: the return of the seasons determined sowing and harvests. The sky was a clock, a calendar, and for many, a message from the gods.

---

### Mesopotamia and Ancient Egypt

The Babylonians (around 2000-500 BCE) were among the first to keep systematic records of celestial motions. They identified the five planets visible to the naked eye - Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn - and developed arithmetic methods to predict their positions.

In Egypt, the orientation of the Giza pyramids reflects a deep knowledge of the sky. The pole star of the time, Thuban, was used as a reference to align monuments with remarkable precision. The Egyptian 365-day calendar was based on the heliacal rising of Sirius, which coincided with the annual flooding of the Nile.

---

### Ancient Greece: the birth of scientific reasoning

In Greece, astronomy made the great leap from empirical observation to geometric modeling of the cosmos.

**Thales of Miletus** (around 624-546 BCE) predicted a solar eclipse and proposed that Earth was a disk floating on water. More importantly, he sought natural explanations for celestial phenomena without relying on myths.

**Pythagoras** (around 570-495 BCE) and his school argued that the sphere was the perfect shape, leading to the idea of a spherical Earth and planets moving on concentric spheres.

**Aristotle** (384-322 BCE) formulated a complete geocentric model: an immobile Earth at the center of the universe, surrounded by crystalline spheres carrying the Moon, the Sun, the planets, and the fixed stars. Although incorrect, this model was coherent and philosophically elegant. It dominated Western thought for nearly two millennia.

**Aristarchus of Samos** (around 310-230 BCE) boldly proposed a heliocentric model - the Sun at the center, with Earth orbiting around it. He also estimated the relative distances of the Moon and the Sun using ingenious geometric methods. His model was largely rejected by his contemporaries, due to the lack of observable proof of Earth's motion.

**Eratosthenes of Cyrene** (276-194 BCE) measured Earth's circumference with astonishing accuracy by comparing the angle of the Sun's shadow in Alexandria and Syene at the summer solstice. He obtained a value of about 40,000 km, very close to the real value.

**Hipparchus** (190-120 BCE) compiled the first star catalog (about 850 stars), discovered the precession of the equinoxes, and introduced the magnitude system to measure stellar brightness. His work became the foundation of positional astronomy for centuries.

---

### Ptolemy and the geocentric system

**Claudius Ptolemy** (around 100-170 CE) synthesized Greek astronomy in his major work, the *Almagest*. His geocentric system used epicycles - small circles on which planets moved, themselves orbiting on larger circles around Earth - to reproduce observed motions, especially the apparent retrograde motion of planets.

This model was functional: it allowed planetary positions to be calculated with enough accuracy for navigation and astrology. The *Almagest* became the dominant reference in astronomy for more than 1300 years.

---

### Arabic and Islamic astronomy (8th-14th century)

While medieval Europe went through a period of intellectual stagnation, the Islamic world preserved and expanded the Greek legacy. Arabic astronomers translated ancient texts, corrected planetary tables, and carried out new observations.

**Al-Battani** (858-929) improved Ptolemy's measurements and accurately determined the obliquity of the ecliptic. **Ibn al-Haytham** (965-1040) criticized the physical inconsistencies of the Ptolemaic model in his *Doubts Concerning Ptolemy*. **Nasir al-Din al-Tusi** (1201-1274) proposed geometric alternatives to epicycles, laying groundwork that later influenced Copernicus.

Many stars still carry Arabic names: Algol, Aldebaran, Betelgeuse, Rigel, Vega - a linguistic legacy of this flourishing period.

---

### Copernicus and the heliocentric revolution

**Nicolaus Copernicus** (1473-1543), a Polish canon, spent decades developing a heliocentric model partly inspired by Aristarchus. In *De revolutionibus orbium coelestium* (1543, published the year he died), he placed the Sun at the center of the planetary system and made Earth an ordinary planet orbiting around it.

The Copernican model naturally explained the planets' apparent retrograde motion - a simple optical effect caused by Earth's orbital overtaking - without relying on Ptolemaic epicycles. However, Copernicus kept circular orbits and still needed geometric corrections to match observations.

The impact of this revolution went beyond astronomy: removing Earth from the center also removed humanity from the center of the universe. The theological and philosophical consequences were immense and sparked a controversy that lasted more than a century.
